# WheelyEasy

2017. A mobile app to help wheelchair users navigate Singapore — find accessible routes, discover wheelchair-friendly venues, and hold businesses accountable for accessibility. Built at Designathon 2017, a hackathon by DesignSingapore Council. Top 7 of 53 teams. Won **Best Business Model**.

## the problem

Wheelchair users in Singapore face a daily information gap. Google Maps might tell you a place exists — but not whether there's a ramp, a lift that works, or a door wide enough to get through. Most accessibility databases are either global and shallow, or local but outdated. There was no hyperlocal, community-driven solution for Singapore.

I spent two weeks navigating Singapore in a wheelchair to understand the problem firsthand — lifts, kerbs, mall entrances, the works. We also user-tested at SPD (Society for the Physically Disabled) and confirmed what we suspected: people were navigating by memory and word of mouth, not any app.

## what we built

WheelyEasy was a mobile app with three stakeholders:

**For users** — find wheelchair-accessible routes and venues in Singapore. Users could also contribute accessibility data and earn vouchers for doing so, creating a self-sustaining community data loop.

**For businesses** — accessibility insights to serve a captive and underserved customer base. We pitched the economic case: wheelchair users and their caregivers represent a loyal, high-frequency segment. Better accessibility multiplies customer lifetime value by 2–3x.

**For government** — anonymised, hyperlocal accessibility data for urban planning. We used SUTD's wheelchair accessibility heat map of Singapore as a baseline.

## positioning

We were uniquely positioned: hyperlocal and routing-focused. Google Maps is global and generic on accessibility. AXSMap, Wheelmate, and other competitors focused on locations but not routing. We sat in the top-right quadrant alone.

The pitch closed on four words: **Simple. Feasible. Validated. Sustainable.**
