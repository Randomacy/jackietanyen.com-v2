[Feature] Awesome Insights in Video Game Charity Awesome Games Done Quick 2020

Jackie Tan
January 17, 2020
ProjectsOfUpLevel

Games Done Quick, or GDQ for short, is a semiannual video game event where gamers play games quickly (or speedrun) to raise money for charitable causes. Awesome Games Done Quick (AGDQ) is held at the start of the year, and Summer Games Done Quick (SGDQ) is held at the beginning of summer.

The speedruns are streamed live online, and donors can donate with comments or requests for challenges in upcoming games in the stream. The first event for 2020 - AGDQ 2020 - was held between January 5 to 12, and raised a record-breaking $3.1M dollars.

Instructor Jackie spent all week watching the video game streams, and was intrigued by the event. More specifically, he was interested in analyzing the data behind the event and whether he could tease out interesting details using his Python skills.

Approach

The project approach was relatively simple - we first scraped all information available on the website, specifically the donation information, followed by data analysis.

In this project, several questions will be explored, for example:

  1. Is there a correlation between game features, e.g., console used, duration of segment, etc, vs donation amounts?

  2. Do people donate more towards the end of the event?

  3. What are the most popular words used in the comment, and do they correlate to anything?

  4. Who raised the most money?

  5. What other insights can we glean from the donation comments?

Data collection

We started off the project by scraping the main site. Fortunately, the site was well-structured, easy to navigate, and data retrieval from the donation table was not challenging.

At the time of scraping, we retrieved 54,100 donation entries from the site. Two rounds of scraping were carried out - first to retrieve the general donation information and second to retrieve comments from donors. We also combined the two datasets by identifying when the donations were made, and matched it to the game that was being streamed at the time.

Data Analysis

#1 Donations over time

Note: Timezone shown is UTC+8, which is different from the timezone of AGDQ 2020

The donation trend was fairly linear for the first six days, followed by a sharp increase in the total amount donated towards the end of the event. This was due to a donation of $356,306 by the Games Done Quick team during the run for Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past Randomizer. The run itself gathered a sizable amount of donations (we’ll talk about that later).

Since the bulk donation by the GDQ team is composed of Twitch subscription, bits (emotes), and sponsorship, that amount was omitted from subsequent analyses as the amount was most likely donated towards the end to build hype and not reflective of the game being played at that time.

#2 Games stats

What were the games that got the most donations? In this analysis, our assumption was that donors donate during the run because of the run alone and not subsequent runes before and after.

After removing the bulk donation by the Games Done Quick team, here’s what we found:

  1. The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past Randomizer (fir_) - $352,484.69

  2. Clone Hero (Guitar Hero) (LT_Humprey) - $263,248.25

  3. Pokemon X/Y (Geeketiquette) - $174,859.67

  4. Katana Zero (SakuraTsubasa) - $131,150.65

  5. Blasphemous (cartridgeblowers) - $108,018.22

How about the duration of the runs? The longest run in the event was Final Fantasy VIII on the Playstation 2 at 9h 10m 18s. The second longest run was half of that, at 4h 4m 1s - Pokemon X/Y on the 3DS. The shortest run was The Adventures of Rocky and Bullwinkle and Friends on the NES, with a run time of 4m 48s.

#3 Consoles used

We plotted a barplot of all the devices used during the runs.

Out of the 139 games, almost one-third of it were PC games and followed by SNES and NES. There were old consoles used, such as the TurboGrafx-16 and MegaDrive (or Sega Genesis) - both of which are three decades old.

#4 Speedrunner stats

In this year’s event, there were a total of 180+ runners for 139 games. Almost all of the speedrunners played only one game in the event.

How about the total amount of time spent speedrunning in the event? We counted only the time spent on speedrunning and not the entire segment which involves the time spent on setting up the game:

  1. Tojju, Muttski, Luzbelheim (Final Fantasy VII) - 33,018 seconds / 550.30 minutes

  2. wartab (Pokemon X/Y) - 14,641 seconds / 244.02 minutes

  3. Reset (Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas) - 14,524 seconds / 242.07 minutes

  4. Andy, Warp World (The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past Randomizer) - 13,260 seconds / 221.00 minutes

  5. Pro_JN, BluntBunny, Cantaloupeme, TGH, Rezovayix, rcdrone, JoshRTA, Greenmario, justinman114 (The Legend of Zelda series / Zelda relay) - 12,072 seconds / 201.2 minutes

What was interesting about the first spot was that it was twice as long as the second spot but the second place only had one player.

We also counted the number of games that each host hosted:

  1. Lizstar - 15 games

  2. YoBGS - 10 games

  3. Patty, Prolix - 9 games

  4. fir_, KrunchyLex - 8 games

  5. Sooraya - 7 games

#5 Identifying correlations

We were interested to see if there were any correlations between the segment length (setup time + speedrun time) and the amount raised.

There does not seem to be any correlation between the run length and the amount of money raised. The trendline is almost vertical as well. How about the amount raised by consoles? We plotted a boxplot to see whether there were segments that raised more money.

The boxplots do not seem to be anything out of the ordinary and they generally do not cross over a particular range of total amount raised. The single lines on the right half of the diagram represent just one console. From the boxplots, we also observe that there seems to be no correlation between the console usage and the total amount of money raised.

At this point we are tempted to say that raising funds through this event is a form of art.

#6 Word cloud

We obtained 36,573 donation comments. Upon text processing, we gathered 681,146 words, and upon counting there were 28,115 unique words.

We built a word cloud using the list of words and we got a pretty awesome word cloud. Most of the comments wish the runners good luck, and there was a fair amount of honking, in reference to the honk that the goose can make in the Untitled Goose Game.

There were also “edf edf”, which refers to the popular chant found in the game Earth Defense Force and is chanted passionately by players of the game. Another common comment element that occurs involve “first time”, most likely shared by first-time donors.

#7 Dedications in comments

Out of curiosity, we wanted to know who these people were donating for. We set up a dictionary that contains keywords for family members and relations, such as "mother", "aunt", and "friend".

Top five people that donors dedicate their donations to:

  1. Friend 😊: 472

  2. Mother 👩: 241

  3. Brother 👦: 163

  4. Father 👨: 162

  5. Grandmother 👵: 149

That’s a lot of donations done on behalf of their friends who presumably have cancer.

Conclusion

We successfully retrieved data from the website, followed by cleaning and merging to obtain a composite dataset that we could use to generate interesting metrics and visualizations. There was no shortage of information to sift through, and we picked several of our favorite visualizations to discuss in this article.

As more and more GDQ events are organized, there will be more chances to derive insights for analysis. Instructor Jackie is already looking forward to the next round of GDQ, which is happening in July later this year.
